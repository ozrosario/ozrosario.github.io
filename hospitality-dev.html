AMPPED RECOMMENDATION

Low-Rate Loan (5.75%) — anchored to a 5–10 year exit

For a partnership targeting a sale in 5–10 years, the Low-Rate 5.75% loan is the strongest structure. It front-loads ~$449,000 more in Year-1 tax benefit than the 9.99% option — value that lands immediately, when the older partners want it — while the planned exit truncates the loan before its higher principal can cost the partnership on the back end. Cash purchase remains the cleanest option if the partnership chooses to self-fund. PACE is conditional on CMBS servicer approval. PPA is shown for completeness but not recommended — it forfeits the tax credits that drive this deal.

Section 02

The Rate Decision — Why 5.75% Wins for an Exit

The two lender options are the same loan with opposite trade-offs: pay a dealer fee to buy down the rate, or keep principal lean and accept a higher rate. For a 5–10 year hold, the math favors the buy-down.

Why 5.75% Beats 9.99% on Exit

Front-loaded benefit+$448,951 more Year-1 federal tax effect — banked in the first filing, before any exit
Lighter carry$23,899/yr less in payments — ~$167K retained over a 7-year hold
Exit truncates the downsideThe 5.75% option's larger principal only hurts over a full 20-yr term. A 5–10 yr sale escapes the back half where that cost lives
Better hold-period NOILower debt service reads better to a buyer underwriting the asset's cash flow

⚠ Flag for the CPA Before Locking

Dealer-fee basisThe $744,892 financed dealer fee appears to inflate the ITC/depreciation basis, driving the higher tax benefit. Confirm the IRS respects dealer-fee-inflated basis before relying on the full $2.44M incentive figure
Rate confirmationProposal states 5.75%; you referenced 5.9%. Confirm the current locked rate with Brad
Payoff at sale5.75% option carries a larger balance ($3.31M vs $2.62M) — more is settled from sale proceeds, but out of value the system itself created
Net read: On a 5–10 year exit, the 5.75% loan captures its front-loaded tax advantage in full while the planned sale cuts off its long-tail interest cost. You get the upside and escape the penalty. The single open item is the CPA's sign-off on dealer-fee basis treatment — if that holds, 5.75% is the clear pick; if the IRS limits basis to actual system cost, the two options converge and the decision shifts back toward the leaner 9.99% principal.

On Cap Rate Selection — Why 6.5% / 7.5% / 8.5%

The bandSouthern California limited-service hotel cap rates currently run roughly 7.5%–9.5%, elevated from the sub-7% era by higher borrowing costs across hospitality
Why not 5–6%A 5–6% cap on an economy/limited-service hotel would be aggressive — closer to trophy multifamily or premium coastal retail. Presenting it risks over-promising if a buyer's appraiser lands at 8.5%
The 6.5% edgeIncluded as the optimistic boundary: a stabilized, energy-cost-insulated asset with a 30-yr solar warranty can justify a premium (lower cap) because the NOI is more predictable and operating risk is lower
Credibility playAnchoring at a defensible band means the real number almost certainly lands inside the range — which builds trust with sophisticated partners rather than eroding it

Modeling note: A buyer's appraiser capitalizes durable, proven NOI. Until the system has an operating production history, expect some discount applied to projected vs. actual savings. Frame these figures as the stabilized value, realized once the system has a metered track record. Conservative cross-check at the modeled $253K offset: $3.9M (6.5%) / $3.4M (7.5%) / $3.0M (8.5%).
