Discovery Q&A Framework — Qualifying Questions by Theme

Theme 01 — QMS Stack
Current Systems & Tools
What does your current QMS stack look like — is it a formal platform (MasterControl, Veeva, Greenlight Guru) or is most of it living in Excel, SharePoint, or Word?
How many systems does a single CAPA touch before it closes — and do those systems talk to each other automatically or does someone bridge them manually?
Where does the Design History File actually live — and who's responsible for keeping it current when design changes happen?
Theme 02 — CAPA Volume & Cycle Time
Operational Throughput
How many CAPAs do you typically have open at any given time, and what's the average time from deviation intake to closure?
When a new CAPA opens — how long does it take before someone has a complete picture of similar historical events to inform the root cause analysis?
Have you ever had an FDA question on a filing that required tracing back to a specific test or complaint — how long did that take and where did the friction come from?
Theme 03 — V&V & Regression Testing
Validation Workflow
When an algorithm parameter or software component changes — what determines whether a full regression suite runs vs. a targeted subset? Is that a documented rule or a judgment call?
On your most recent 510(k) engagement — how many bench test cycles did it take, and where did the most unbillable engineering hours go?
How do you currently compare new device performance against your predicate — is that a manual side-by-side or is there tooling that tracks bias, SD, and LOA automatically?
Theme 04 — Team Structure & Regulatory History
Org Context & FDA Experience
Who owns quality system compliance day-to-day — is it a dedicated QA role, or does it fall on engineering or program management alongside other responsibilities?
Have you had an FDA 483 observation or audit finding related to CAPA documentation, traceability, or quality system management — and what was the corrective path?
If you're running multiple client engagements concurrently — where does the quality system become the constraint on how many you can take on?

Objection & Barrier Navigation — Pre-armed Responses

⚠
“Any AI tool touching our quality system must be validated under 21 CFR Part 11.”
Validation Rigor
Their Concern
Computer Software Assurance (CSA) requirements mean any tool that touches regulated records must be validated. That validation effort can cost as much as the tool itself and takes months.
The Response
The agent doesn't touch your validated system of record — it reads from it and queues drafts for human review. Under CSA guidelines, decision-support tools with HITL oversight have a significantly lower validation burden than automated execution tools. We're in the Safe Zone deliberately. Your QA team approves every output before it enters any Part 11-regulated record.
⚠
“What happens if the AI hallucinates an ISO 13485 clause or FDA guidance?”
Hallucination Risk
Their Concern
If an AI fabricates a regulatory citation, the resulting compliance violation can halt a product launch. This is the highest trust barrier in regulated environments.
The Response
RAG is non-negotiable in our architecture. The agent is strictly tethered to your validated SOPs, ISO documentation, and the FDA database. It cannot cite what isn't in the approved repository. If the answer isn't there, the agent escalates to a human — it doesn't guess. This is architected specifically to solve the hallucination problem, not ignore it.
⚠
“Our IT and security team will never allow read access to our QMS or PLM data.”
Data Silos / IT Security
Their Concern
Engineering works in fast-moving tools. QA lives in rigid, document-heavy validated environments. Connecting them requires breaking through IT security red tape and data governance policies.
The Response
MCP gateways (Tyk.io, Snowflake managed MCP) enforce strict Identity and Access Management (IAM) controls natively. The agent gets read-only, scoped access — never write access to validated systems without HITL. Every data access is logged for audit. This architecture was designed to satisfy IT security and Quality teams simultaneously. We start with a sandboxed pilot — no production system access required to prove value.
⚠
“Change control in our QMS would require us to document and validate any new tooling.”
Change Control Burden
Their Concern
Introducing any new tool into a regulated workflow requires going through a formal change control process — which takes time, resources, and creates risk of disruption during active product development.
The Response
The overlay doesn't modify your QMS — it reads from it. From a change control perspective, a read-only AI agent with HITL review is categorically different from modifying a validated system. We structure the pilot explicitly to stay outside your validated system boundary — which means no change control trigger on the QMS side. The output the agent produces is drafted content reviewed and approved by your QA team before it ever enters a regulated record.
⚠
“Our quality team will never trust an AI to handle anything CAPA-related.”
Cultural Change Management
Their Concern
Quality professionals are trained to be risk-averse. Asking them to trust an autonomous agent to handle regulated compliance activities requires a profound cultural shift that takes time and evidence.
The Response
We're not asking anyone to trust the AI. We're asking them to review a draft that took 48 hours instead of 3 weeks to produce. The HITL model means the quality team's judgment is still the final gate — every time, without exception. Start with the lowest-risk workflow (clustering a CAPA backlog, not closing active CAPAs). Let the output speak. Trust follows demonstrated accuracy, not promises.
